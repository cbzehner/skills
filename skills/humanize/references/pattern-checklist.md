# Pattern Checklist

Use this as an audit aid, not a replacement table. Context wins.

## Filler Phrases

- "It's worth noting that..." / "It's important to note that..."
- "Let's dive into..." / "Let's explore..." / "Let's unpack that"
- "Here's the thing..." / "Here's why it matters..."
- "In today's fast-paced world..." / "In today's digital landscape..."
- "Without further ado..."
- "At the end of the day..."
- "It goes without saying..."
- "When it comes to..."
- "In the realm of..."

Usually delete these. If the sentence needs a transition, write the transition in plain words.

## Structural Patterns

- **"Not just X, it's Y"** — The most overused LLM rhetorical pattern. Rewrite as a direct statement.
- **"More X than Y"** — Often a fake insight. State the actual quality instead of negating its neighbor.
- **Announcement-colon openers** — "The key insight:" / "The issue:" / "The approach:" before the thought. Usually the label can go.
- **Mini-aphorism closers** — "That's the whole thing." / "That's what changed." Delete unless the preceding paragraph earned it.
- **Em dash overload** — One or two per page can be fine. Several in one paragraph is a tell. Replace with a period, comma, parenthetical, or a rewritten sentence. Vary the fix.
- **Transition word chains** — "However... Furthermore... Additionally... Moreover..." at paragraph openings. Start paragraphs with subjects instead.
- **Uniform paragraph/sentence length** — Human writing is lumpy: short punchy sentences mixed with longer ones. Vary deliberately.
- **Sycophantic openers** — "Great question!", "Excellent point!", "That's a really interesting..." — delete.
- **The false-depth pattern** — Restates the problem in fancier words, lists obvious considerations, concludes vaguely. Replace with specifics, data, edge cases.
- **List symmetry addiction** — Exactly 3 or 5 items, all same grammatical structure. Real lists are uneven.
- **Over-smooth paragraph arcs** — Every paragraph opens with a claim and closes with a tidy lesson. Let one sentence do real work, not recap.

## Punctuation Tells

- **Semicolon overuse** — Treat semicolons as suspicious in ordinary technical and product prose. Use a period or conjunction unless the register really wants one.
- **Mid-sentence colon** — "The answer: start smaller." Prefer "Start smaller." or "The answer is to start smaller."
- **Colon overuse in headings** — "Topic: The Subtitle Pattern" repeated. Vary heading styles.
- **Double em dash wrapping** — "X — like this — Y" is rarely needed. Use parentheses or split the sentence.
- **Hedging chains** — "can," "may," "might," "could potentially," "it's possible that." State facts or admit uncertainty directly.

## Register Tells

- **README/docs**: generic value props, "powerful", "seamless", "robust", long install prose, repeated section templates.
- **Email**: "I hope this finds you well", "just wanted to reach out", "happy to discuss further", "looking forward to connecting".
- **Slack/chat**: polished paragraphs wearing lowercase, emoji, or "lmk" as a costume.
- **Technical writing**: "developers should consider", "valuable insights", "critical aspect", "best practices" without concrete tools or failure modes.
- **Marketing copy**: "unlock the full potential", "transform the way", "elevate", "game-changing", "next-generation".
