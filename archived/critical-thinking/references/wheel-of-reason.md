## Overview

The Wheel of Reason teaches the Elements (or Parts) of Thinking. It helps develop the ability to analyze articles, essays, books, texts of any kind, professional and academic subjects, concepts, work products and constructs, as well as problems and issues in professional or personal life.

The Paul-Elder framework has three interlocking components:

- **Elements of Thought** — the parts present in all reasoning
- **Intellectual Standards** — the criteria for evaluating the quality of each element
- **Intellectual Traits** — the dispositions of character that develop through consistent application

The metaphor: Elements are the *anatomy* of thought, Standards are the *health metrics* you apply to each organ, and Traits are the *fitness* that emerges from disciplined practice.

## The Eight Elements of Thought

These eight elements exist in ALL reasoning, by ALL persons, at ALL times. They form equal sectors of a circle (the Wheel). Analysis proceeds by "going around the circle" to appreciate each element individually and collectively.

### 1. Purpose (Goal, Objective)
**What it addresses:** The goal or objective the thinker pursues.
**Key principle:** All reasoning has a purpose.
**Guiding questions:**
- What exactly am I trying to accomplish?
- What is my fundamental purpose?
- What is the purpose of this text/argument/policy?
- Is my purpose clearly stated or hidden?
- Do I have multiple purposes? Are they consistent or in tension?
- Am I clear about my long-term and short-term purposes?

### 2. Question at Issue (Problem, Issue)
**What it addresses:** The problem, issue, or question that the reasoning attempts to settle.
**Key principle:** All reasoning attempts to figure something out, settle some question, solve some problem.
**Guiding questions:**
- What question am I trying to answer?
- What problem am I trying to solve?
- What issue am I trying to resolve?
- Is the question clear and precisely stated?
- Does the question contain sub-questions that need to be addressed first?
- Is this a question with a definitive answer, or one that calls for reasoned judgment among multiple defensible positions?

### 3. Information (Data, Facts, Observations, Experiences)
**What it addresses:** The data, evidence, observations, and experiences used in reasoning.
**Key principle:** All reasoning uses some information, data, or evidence.
**Guiding questions:**
- What information do I need to answer this question?
- What data bear on this issue?
- What experience informs my thinking here?
- Is this information accurate?
- Have I gathered sufficient information?
- Have I ignored information that contradicts my position?
- What information would I need to change my mind?

### 4. Interpretation and Inference (Conclusions, Solutions)
**What it addresses:** The conclusions, solutions, and interpretations drawn from the information.
**Key principle:** All reasoning contains inferences or interpretations by which we draw conclusions and give meaning to data.
**Guiding questions:**
- What conclusions can I draw from this information?
- Are there alternative interpretations of this data?
- Does the evidence actually support this conclusion?
- Am I making any inferences that go beyond what the data support?
- Are my inferences consistent with each other?
- What is the most reasonable inference given all the evidence?

### 5. Concepts (Theories, Definitions, Axioms, Laws, Principles, Models)
**What it addresses:** The ideas, theories, laws, principles, or models used to make sense of things.
**Key principle:** All reasoning is expressed through, and shaped by, concepts and ideas.
**Guiding questions:**
- What is the most basic concept at work here?
- What theories am I using?
- Am I using these concepts appropriately?
- Are there alternative concepts I should consider?
- Am I distorting any concepts to serve my interests?
- Do I need to clarify or define any concepts before proceeding?

### 6. Assumptions (Presuppositions, Axioms, Taking for Granted)
**What it addresses:** The beliefs taken for granted—the starting points of reasoning.
**Key principle:** All reasoning rests upon assumptions.
**Guiding questions:**
- What am I taking for granted?
- What assumptions underlie my reasoning?
- Are my assumptions justified by the evidence?
- What would happen if my assumptions turned out to be wrong?
- Am I making assumptions I shouldn't?
- Are there hidden assumptions in this argument?

### 7. Implications and Consequences
**What it addresses:** Where the reasoning leads—what follows from it.
**Key principle:** All reasoning leads somewhere and has implications and consequences.
**Guiding questions:**
- What are the implications of my reasoning?
- What consequences follow from my conclusions?
- If people accepted my reasoning, what would follow?
- Am I considering all the significant consequences?
- Are there unintended implications I have overlooked?
- What are the implications if I am wrong?

### 8. Point of View (Frame of Reference, Perspective, Orientation)
**What it addresses:** The perspective, frame of reference, or vantage point from which the reasoning proceeds.
**Key principle:** All reasoning occurs from some point of view.
**Guiding questions:**
- What is my point of view?
- Am I considering other relevant viewpoints?
- Am I fair in how I present opposing viewpoints?
- What would someone who disagrees with me say?
- What perspective am I most sympathetic to, and why?
- Am I stuck in one point of view when the issue calls for multiple?

## The Nine Intellectual Standards

These standards evaluate the *quality* of reasoning. Apply them to each Element of Thought.

### 1. Clarity (the gateway standard)
**Definition:** Reasoning you can understand; free from confusion or ambiguity.
**Why it matters:** If a statement lacks clarity, you cannot determine whether it holds accuracy or relevance.
**Checking questions:**
- Could you elaborate further?
- Could you give me an example?
- Could you illustrate what you mean?
- Could you express that point in another way?

### 2. Accuracy
**Definition:** Free from errors, mistakes, or distortions; true to the facts.
**Checking questions:**
- How could we check on that?
- How could we find out if that holds true?
- How could we verify or test that?

### 3. Precision
**Definition:** Exact, specific, and detailed to the degree required.
**Checking questions:**
- Could you be more specific?
- Could you give me more details?
- Could you be more exact?

### 4. Relevance
**Definition:** Bearing upon the matter at hand; each part makes a difference.
**Checking questions:**
- How does that relate to the problem?
- How does that bear on the question?
- How does that help us with the issue?

### 5. Depth
**Definition:** Thorough engagement with the complexities of the issue.
**Checking questions:**
- What factors make this a difficult problem?
- What are some of the complexities of this question?
- What are some of the difficulties we need to deal with?

### 6. Breadth
**Definition:** Multiple viewpoints and perspectives considered.
**Checking questions:**
- Do we need to look at this from another perspective?
- Do we need to consider another point of view?
- Is there another way to look at this question?

### 7. Logic (Logicalness)
**Definition:** Reasoning that holds together; conclusions follow from the evidence; no internal contradictions.
**Checking questions:**
- Does all of this make sense together?
- Does the first paragraph fit with the last?
- Does what you say follow from the evidence?
- Are there any contradictions in this reasoning?

### 8. Significance
**Definition:** Focus on what matters most; central rather than peripheral.
**Checking questions:**
- Is this the most important problem to consider?
- Is this the central idea to focus on?
- Which of these facts carry the most weight?
- Does this point matter in the larger picture?

### 9. Fairness
**Definition:** Justifiable in context; free from bias, distortion, or self-serving manipulation.
**Checking questions:**
- Is my thinking justifiable in context?
- Am I taking into account the thinking of others?
- Is my purpose fair given the situation?
- Am I using concepts appropriately, or distorting them to get what I want?
- Do I have a vested interest in this issue?

## The Eight Intellectual Traits

These character dispositions develop through habitual application of the Standards to the Elements. They describe the fair-minded critical thinker.

### 1. Intellectual Humility
Awareness of the limits of one's knowledge. Sensitivity to bias, prejudice, and limitations in one's viewpoint. Not claiming more than one actually knows.

### 2. Intellectual Courage
Willingness to challenge popular or long-held beliefs when evidence warrants it. Readiness to entertain ideas that provoke negative reactions. Not passively accepting what one has "always believed."

### 3. Intellectual Empathy
Capacity to reconstruct the viewpoints and reasoning of others accurately. Willingness to imagine yourself in others' positions. Recognizing that understanding does not require agreement.

### 4. Intellectual Autonomy
Independent thinking; mastery of one's own reasoning processes. Not uncritically accepting the beliefs of others. Analyzing claims on their merits rather than deferring to authority.

### 5. Intellectual Integrity
Holding oneself to the same rigorous standards of evidence and proof that one demands of others. Honest acknowledgment of inconsistencies in one's own thought and action.

### 6. Intellectual Perseverance
Struggling through difficulty, confusion, and frustration to gain genuine understanding. Not giving up on hard intellectual problems prematurely.

### 7. Confidence in Reason
Trust that one's own higher interests and those of humankind are best served by giving the freest play to reason. Encouragement of people to come to their own conclusions through their own rational faculties.

### 8. Fair-Mindedness
Treating all viewpoints alike, without reference to one's own feelings or vested interests. Adherence to intellectual standards without reference to one's own advantage.

```
     ┌──────────────────────────────────────────┐
     │       INTELLECTUAL STANDARDS              │
     │   (the quality criteria)                  │
     │                                           │
     │   Clarity, Accuracy, Precision,           │
     │   Relevance, Depth, Breadth,              │
     │   Logic, Significance, Fairness           │
     └─────────────────┬────────────────────────┘
                       │
              applied systematically
              to...
                       │
                       ▼
     ┌──────────────────────────────────────────┐
     │        ELEMENTS OF THOUGHT                │
     │   (the anatomy of reasoning)              │
     │                                           │
     │   Purpose, Question, Information,         │
     │   Inference, Concepts, Assumptions,       │
     │   Implications, Point of View             │
     └──────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The formula: **Apply the Standards to the Elements, and Traits develop over time.**

For any Element, you can ask any Standard's questions. Example: for the element "Assumptions," you can ask clarity questions ("Can I state my assumptions clearly?"), accuracy questions ("Are my assumptions accurate?"), relevance questions ("Are all my assumptions relevant to the issue at hand?"), and so on.

## Barriers to Critical Thinking

### Egocentric Thinking
The natural human tendency to see the world from a narrow self-serving perspective. Manifests as:
- Assuming one's beliefs need no justification
- Interpreting information in self-serving ways
- Confusing what one wants to believe with what evidence supports

### Sociocentric Thinking
Egocentric thinking writ large—the uncritical absorption of group beliefs. Manifests as:
- Assuming the beliefs of one's group need no justification
- Viewing the world from the group's perspective as though it were the only reasonable one
- Dismissing outgroup perspectives without genuine consideration

### Overcoming These Barriers
The intellectual traits (especially humility, empathy, and fair-mindedness) serve as antidotes. The disciplined application of the Wheel of Reason to one's own thinking exposes egocentric and sociocentric patterns.

## The Logic-of-X Template

Use this template to analyze the logic of any text, argument, subject, or situation:

1. **The main purpose of X is...**
2. **The key question X addresses is...**
3. **The most important information in X is...**
4. **The main inferences/conclusions in X are...**
5. **The key concept(s) we need to understand in X are...**
6. **The main assumption(s) underlying X are...**
7. **The main implication(s) of X (if we take it seriously) are...**
8. **The main point(s) of view in X are...**

## Wheel of Reason Activities

The Critical Thinking Academy's Wheel of Reason section offers structured activities:

1. **Analyze an Article/Essay/Text** — Apply the eight elements to dissect the reasoning in any written work
2. **Analyze a Subject/Discipline** — Understand the logic of an academic or professional domain (e.g., the logic of biology, the logic of economics)
3. **Analyze a Problem/Issue** — Break down personal or professional problems using the elements
4. **Analyze Concepts** — Examine the concepts you use to make sure you understand and apply them faithfully
5. **Analyze an Author's Reasoning** — Reconstruct an author's reasoning by identifying each element in their work

The Criteria Corner (a companion section) teaches the Universal Standards for evaluating each element.

Each activity follows the same pattern: go around the wheel element by element, applying the relevant standards, and building a comprehensive analysis.
