# design-precedent-study

Study strong design precedents, extract the underlying moves, and translate them into original directions for the current product or service.

## When to Use

- The user asks for inspiration, references, moodboards, competitive analysis, examples to emulate, or "great artists steal" design thinking.
- A design direction feels generic and needs better taste.
- The team wants to understand what excellent products, publications, services, or public-interest sites do well.
- A prototype should be grounded in known successful patterns instead of invented from scratch.

## When NOT to Use

- The user wants to copy a specific site's visual identity, brand, illustration, layout, or proprietary assets.
- The user already has a precise direction and only needs implementation.
- The problem is accessibility conformance or flow repair, not reference gathering.

## What to Skip

- Do not clone, trace, or closely imitate another product's distinctive expression.
- Do not use references as decoration. Extract mechanics: hierarchy, pacing, affordance, language, trust, recovery, density, and service shape.
- Do not overfit to trendy examples when boring web standards would serve the user better.

## Workflow

1. Define the design question.
   State what the precedent study must answer: trust, density, onboarding, editorial clarity, civic legitimacy, conversion, community, moderation, or service recovery.

2. Choose reference categories.
   Look beyond direct competitors. Include adjacent domains, public institutions, editorial products, old web patterns, service organizations, forms, tools, and high-quality mundane sites.

3. Analyze, do not copy.
   For each reference, extract reusable principles: information hierarchy, navigation model, content rhythm, interaction pattern, tone, evidence of trust, and failure handling.

4. Separate surface from structure.
   Surface is color, typeface, imagery, and brand styling. Structure is the move worth borrowing. Prefer borrowing structure.

5. Translate into original directions.
   Recombine principles for the current audience, content, constraints, and service promise. Make the result feel native to this product.

6. Identify anti-patterns.
   Note what not to borrow: deceptive urgency, over-designed cards, inaccessible contrast, JS-only interactions, vanity animation, vague enterprise language.

7. Produce a reference brief.
   Summarize sources, extracted principles, candidate directions, and concrete next design moves.

## Output Shape

- Design question
- Reference set
- What each reference does well
- Principles to steal
- Surface details to avoid copying
- Candidate directions
- Next prototype or critique steps

## Examples

- "Find inspiration for a civic signup flow" -> study government services, libraries, mutual aid, healthcare intake, and trustworthy editorial forms.
- "This community dashboard feels generic" -> study moderation tools, forums, admin consoles, public-good dashboards, and newspaper information hierarchy.
- "How should an appeals service feel?" -> study legal aid, benefits applications, support escalation, case tracking, and complaint resolution services.

## Failure Handling

If external research is unavailable, work from known categories and ask the user to provide examples later. If a requested reference is too close to copying, explain the risk and extract only general principles.
